# few points

## regarding textbook

- this textbook is the archetypal product of the _Mainland Chinese language standardization project_, designed to teach the Beijing-accent, so you'll find more érhuà and neutral tones as compared to _Guóyǔ_ (Taiwan) books, which use traditional characters and have less _érhuà_.
- I've tried to consolidate important concepts scattered across the textbook and linked them internally
- just buy Pleco and especially the Outlier Dictionary addon (you'll thank me later)  
- unfortunately, this textbook was last revised on June 1989, so you are stuck with some words whose meanings have changed completely, to the point that they are now offensive.
- even stroke orders for some characters are outdated as compared to the 2013 《通用规范汉字表》. This means, orders you see on Pleco (or any online resource for that matter) is significantly different. I learnt the latest standards and couldn't care less what the old book said.
- start mogging HSK 1 words with Anki (though I used flashcards on Pleco itself) For Anki, I used cards from here: https://github.com/krmanik/Anki-xiehanzi
- pinyin is extremely important to disambiguate homophones
- I have also added my Pleco flashcards as XML, import 'em. It covers all the words given in this course book. Grind it to the core by not just recalling pronunciations, but also writing the characters and pinyin on a grid notebook initially
- fit characters into equal sized grids (no matter how many strokes they have)
- this textbook should cover HSK.v2 HSK-1 and some HSK-2 words
- there are many `Chinese comprehensive input` videos on youtube, use them as well
- not everything is 1:1 mapped, some concepts are linked with each other and appropriate lesson number is tagged

>[!CAUTION]
>Never rely on `ChatGPT` as it is not pedantic and correct when it comes to Chinese grammar, always se Chinese frontier models  such as `Deepseek` or `Qwen`.

>[!TIP] 
>If you use any harness like `OpenCode`, create a custom GPT/agent stuffed with the flashcard XML and the textbook PDF in its context.
>This way, you can query doubts or generate more practice exercises limited to the course.

## regarding course

- usually there are 2 sections at St. Stephen's College, I was enrolled in section a.
- you’ll be told to repeat Chinese characters and solve textbook exercise in a grid notebook. However, I repeated characters in a grid notebook and solved exercises in a ruled notebook (answer sheets will be ruled, so acclimatize early on to ruled notebooks)
- for the first few chapters use 0.5 mm lead pencil, then switch writing with pen. I used Uniball Zento 0.5mm but I recommend 0.3 mm (that blue ink is especially vibrant)
- ive tried to include every important point from each lesson, however, I might have missed some unique sentences, so thoroughly supplement my notes with the textbook
- pay close attention to later chapters
- once you complete chapter 15, start self assessing using [[Resources#few past year papers|internal assessment papers]]
- make your own self-intro that must cover important grammar points from each lesson
- your instructor on your first day should provide you the following information about course structure, though it may change.
- you'll be considered to have passed if you secure at least 200 scores in total, or a distinction for 300+ scores.
- lastly, make your own notes as well, this way you'll actually go through each lesson and internalize things that are not readily digestible by a superfluous go-through.
